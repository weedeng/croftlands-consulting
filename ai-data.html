The Capability Stack

Three Ways AI Changes What a CFO Can Deliver

Each capability below maps a specific machine learning technique to a finance outcome — with the methodology, the tools and the business context all understood by the same person deploying it.

Predictive Analytics

XGBoost Random Forest Logistic Regression scikit-learn

Classification models trained on historical customer data to predict future behaviour. In a SaaS context this means a weekly churn risk score per account — a probability that a given customer will cancel in the next 30, 60 or 90 days. Features include login frequency, feature adoption rate, support ticket volume, payment behaviour and contract age. XGBoost and Random Forest consistently outperform simpler models on real SaaS datasets.

The same framework applies to attrition risk for PE portfolio companies, lead conversion probability from CRM data, and loan default risk in financial services. The model output is always actionable: a ranked list of accounts or individuals requiring intervention, with the probability score quantifying urgency.

Customer Intelligence

K-Means Hierarchical Clustering DBSCAN PCA t-SNE

Unsupervised learning that finds natural structure in customer data without telling the algorithm what to look for. K-Means and hierarchical clustering reveal segments based on behaviour, contract value, product usage mix or acquisition channel. The resulting segments drive pricing strategy (which tier to price up), upsell targeting (which segment is ready for expansion), and renewal prioritisation (which segment is habitually at risk).

PCA and t-SNE reduce high-dimensional customer feature sets to two or three dimensions for visualisation — making segments legible in board presentations rather than buried in a spreadsheet. Applied to AllLife Bank customer segmentation and marketing campaign analysis in MIT capstone work.

Generative AI & LLM Pipelines

Claude API OpenAI GPT Zero-Shot Few-Shot Chain-of-Thought

Large language models applied to finance document workflows. The most mature use case is automated management commentary: actuals data is extracted from the ERP, variance calculations are computed in Python, and the Claude or OpenAI API generates the narrative — month-end commentary, variance explanations, board pack summaries — ready for CFO review. The finance team edits rather than writes from scratch.

Beyond reporting, LLM pipelines handle document summarisation (FDD reports, board papers, contracts), financial query answering on structured data, and quality assessment using an LLM-as-judge framework. Prompt engineering patterns — zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought — are tuned to the finance domain for reliable, consistent output. Demonstrated in the MIT capstone Real-Time Retail Feedback Intelligence project.

In Practice

How These Models Deploy in Client Engagements

Each example below is a concrete deployment — not a proof of concept, but a working system built alongside CFO-level finance leadership in a real business.

SaaS Churn Risk Scoring

A Random Forest or XGBoost classifier is trained on 12–24 months of customer behaviour data: login frequency, feature adoption, support ticket volume, payment behaviour and contract age. The model is re-scored weekly and produces a churn probability per account.

The output is a ranked list of at-risk customers — not a chart, but a prioritised intervention queue for the customer success team. The customer who was 8% risk last week and is 34% this week gets a call before the one who has been flat at 15% for a month.

Typical result: 3–6 weeks of advance warning on accounts that would otherwise churn without notice.

LLM Board Pack Commentary

Actuals data is extracted from the ERP via API or CSV export. Python calculates the variances — actual vs. budget, actual vs. prior year, current month vs. prior month. These variances are passed to the Claude or OpenAI API with a finance-domain system prompt and few-shot examples of acceptable commentary style.

The API returns a first draft of the management commentary section — department by department, variance by variance. The CFO edits rather than writes from scratch, saving 2–4 hours per month-end cycle and improving consistency across reporting periods.

From raw actuals to reviewed draft commentary in under 20 minutes.
