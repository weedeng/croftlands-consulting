From Excel to ARIMA: Upgrading Your 12-Week Cashflow Model

Why manual spreadsheet extrapolation is the wrong tool for cashflow forecasting — and how ARIMA and SARIMAX time-series models produce statistically-grounded forecasts with confidence intervals that a board can actually use.

The 12-week rolling cashflow model is one of the most important outputs a finance function produces. It is what lenders look at when they are deciding whether to support you through a tight period. It is what PE investors are watching when a portfolio company hits a covenant. It is the model that, in a crisis, tells you exactly how many weeks you have left.

Most of these models are built in Excel and updated manually. Actuals are pasted in from the bank reconciliation each Monday morning, future weeks are extrapolated by hand from the latest assumptions, and the forecast is signed off by the CFO in time for the weekly call. This works — until it doesn’t. This article explains why time-series modelling with ARIMA and SARIMAX produces a more reliable forecast, and what the implementation looks like in practice.

The Problem with Manual Extrapolation

A traditional cashflow model works on a receipts-and-payments basis: you list every expected inflow and outflow, week by week, and net them to get the closing cash position. The challenge is not the structure — it is the forecast of future receipts and payments. Most finance teams handle this in one of two ways:

  • Rule-of-thumb extrapolation: “Last month we collected £420k, so we’ll forecast £420k again.” Or: “Collections average 45 days, so invoice value from 6 weeks ago flows through now.” These rules capture the right intuition but are not statistically calibrated to the actual pattern of collections in this business.
  • Assumption-driven modelling: Every cell in the forecast contains a human assumption. The problem is that assumptions accumulate — by week 10 of a 12-week model, you are stacking assumption upon assumption, and the forecast error compounds accordingly. No one knows what the error bands are.

Neither approach is wrong — they are rational given the tools typically available. The issue is that they are not grounded in the statistical properties of the underlying time series. ARIMA-based forecasting is.

What ARIMA Actually Does

ARIMA stands for AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average. It is a family of models that describes a time series purely in terms of its own past values — no external inputs required. The three components are:

  • AR (AutoRegressive): The value this week is a weighted function of values from previous weeks. For weekly cashflow collections, there is typically a strong AR component — a week of high collections tends to be followed by a slightly-above-average week as the underlying commercial activity continues.
  • I (Integrated): The differencing order — how many times you need to difference the series to make it stationary (to remove trend). Most cashflow series need one round of differencing (d=1).
  • MA (Moving Average): The forecast also adjusts for recent forecast errors. If the model consistently under-predicted last week, the MA component corrects for this.

The output of an ARIMA model is not a single number — it is a probability distribution over possible future values. That gives you a point forecast (the most likely outcome) and confidence intervals (the range within which actual collections are statistically likely to fall). This is the key advantage over manual extrapolation: the model tells you how uncertain it is.

Adding Seasonality: SARIMAX

For events businesses, and for many SaaS businesses with annual contract cycles, cashflow has a seasonal pattern that a standard ARIMA model cannot capture. SARIMAX (Seasonal ARIMA with eXogenous regressors) extends the model to handle this.

For an events business, the seasonal component is obvious: receipts spike in the weeks before a major event and collapse in the weeks after. A SARIMAX model fitted to two years of weekly cash data will learn this seasonal pattern and incorporate it into the forecast automatically — so the week-8 forecast for a period that includes a major event is not the same as the week-8 forecast for a quiet period.

The eXogenous regressors (the X in SARIMAX) allow you to add external drivers to the model. For events, the most useful exogenous variable is forward bookings — the value of confirmed ticket or exhibitor sales for future events. This turns the model from a purely historical time-series extrapolation into a hybrid: statistical pattern from history, plus the specific forward pipeline that finance and sales already track.

Implementation: What It Looks Like in Python

The implementation uses the statsmodels library in Python, which provides both ARIMA and SARIMAX through a clean API.

The workflow has four stages:

  1. Stationarity testing. Before fitting an ARIMA model, the series must be stationary — no trend, constant variance. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test checks this statistically and tells you how many times to difference the series. For most cashflow series, d=1 is sufficient.
  2. Order selection. The p (AR order) and q (MA order) parameters are selected by examining the autocorrelation function (ACF) and partial autocorrelation function (PACF) plots, or by minimising AIC (Akaike Information Criterion) across a grid of candidate models. In practice, for weekly cashflow data, p=1 and q=1 are often sufficient starting points.
  3. Model fitting. statsmodels fits the model to the historical data using maximum likelihood estimation. The fit object includes diagnostic statistics: residuals should be white noise (no autocorrelation), and Ljung-Box test p-values should be above 0.05.
  4. Forecast generation. The .get_forecast(steps=12) method produces 12 weeks of forecasts with confidence intervals at any specified confidence level (80%, 95% etc.). These can be extracted as a Pandas DataFrame and written directly into the cashflow model template.

Practical calibration tip: Walk-forward validation is the right way to evaluate a time-series model. Fit the model on data through week T, forecast weeks T+1 through T+12, and compare forecasts to actuals. Then advance T by one week and repeat. This gives you a distribution of forecast errors at each horizon — the most important output for setting realistic confidence intervals. A model that looks good in-sample can still be a poor forecaster; walk-forward validation tells you what it actually achieves on unseen data.

The Output: What Changes for Finance and the Board

The practical output difference between a manual model and an ARIMA-based model is significant — and most visible at the board level.

A manual model presents a single line: week 1 = £380k inflows, week 2 = £410k, and so on to week 12. Every number is a point estimate. The board either trusts it or doesn’t. When actuals diverge from forecast, the finance team explains why — but there is no prior statement of how uncertain the forecast was to begin with.

An ARIMA-based model presents three lines: the central forecast, plus the 80% confidence band. Week 6 central forecast: £420k. 80% confidence interval: £310k–£530k. This is genuinely useful information. If the lower bound of the confidence interval at week 8 puts closing cash below the covenant threshold, you can flag that risk in the current board meeting — weeks before the actual problem would emerge. That is what proactive risk management looks like.

The second change is in the update cycle. A manual model requires someone to sit down each Monday and re-enter assumptions for every future week. An ARIMA-based model re-fits itself automatically when new actuals are available — the Python script runs, the new data is appended, and updated forecasts with revised confidence intervals are produced in minutes. The finance team’s time is freed from maintaining the model to interpreting its output.

What This Does Not Replace

ARIMA and SARIMAX model the statistical patterns in historical data. They cannot predict events that have no precedent in the history — a contract cancellation from the business’s single largest customer, a sudden change in payment terms imposed by a key supplier, or a new revenue stream not yet in the data. For these, human judgment and assumption-driven scenario modelling remain essential.

The right architecture is a hybrid: the ARIMA model handles the baseline forecast for the regular pattern of receipts and payments (direct debits, payroll, typical supplier payments, normal collections), while the finance team overlays specific large known items — facility drawdowns, large contract receipts, capital expenditure — as manual adjustments. The statistical model handles the noise; the human handles the signal.

This combination — automated statistical baseline plus structured human overlays — consistently outperforms both pure manual forecasting and naive statistical extrapolation. It is also more defensible: when the board asks why the cashflow forecast changed, the answer is “the model has updated based on 12 months of new actuals, and here is the specific customer receipt that drove the change in week 8” — not “we adjusted our assumptions.”
